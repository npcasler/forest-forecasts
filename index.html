Why Forest Forecasts?

Forests in western North America are important from hydrological, commercial, ecological, and cultural points of view. Throughout the west, human populations depend on snow-pack in forested areas for drinking water. Forested areas literally act as “water towers” for near-by lower-elevation, semi-arid and arid areas where millions live. Recent studies show that forests in western North America are undergoing profound change: large-scale forest die-offs have been documented1, as well as background mortality rates twice their historical levels2. This mortality is associated with climate change, via elevated temperatures and consequently increased drought stress, as well as insect outbreaks1,3-6. Insect outbreaks4 and fire7 are influenced by climate, thus climate change is both a direct and indirect driver of forest stress. The consensus from recent models is that about half the coniferous forest area in western North America will be lost to climate change over the course of the twenty-first century, from 24% of the total area, to 11%, with much of this area replaced by shrub or grasslands8.


We need new ways of visualizing and communicating about the changes happening in western forests, because they are occurring on a scale that is continental, and the consequences for human well-being are pervasive. For the sake of all the benefits that forests offer - ecosystem services, renewable natural resources (water, wood), recreation, as well as natural and cultural heritage - we are in urgent need of forecasts of their response to climate change. These visualization tools allow us to see what the future might realistically look like; they also stimulate conversations about what we want to do – what actions we might take to influence what that future will look like.


Species

We selected nine species that are dominant in forests of the Rocky Mountain region and throughout western North America, across a range of forest types. These include woodland species like Rocky Mountain juniper, Gambel oak, and Piñon pine, mid-elevation species like Ponderosa pine and Douglas-fir, and high elevation species like Aspen, Engelmann spruce, Lodgepole pine, and Subalpine fir.

Climate Change Scenarios

Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs)

Our forecasts are based on three carbon scenarios - called "representative concentration pathways" (rcp's) - used by all the major climate modeling research centers around the world (CMIP5). These rcp's are greenhouse gas trajectories that describe possible future climates, depending on how much greenhouse gases are emitted in the coming years. The rcp's (2.6, 4.5, 8.5) are named after the expected increase in radiative forcing in 2100 compared to pre-industrial values, in units of watts per meter squared (W/m2).

Best Case Emission Scenario

Emission pathway is representative of scenarios where humanity is able to lower greenhouse gas concentration levels. It is a “peak-and-decline” scenario where we start reducing greenhouse gas emissions drastically. Here the impact of greenhouse gas emissions reach their peak by mid-century, and the decrease and eventually returns to "2.6 W/m2" by 2100. In order to reach such radiative forcing levels, greenhouse gas emissions (and indirectly emissions of air pollutants) are reduced substantially, over time (Van Vuuren et al. 2007a).

Stabilization Scenario

Greenhouse emissions are stabilized shortly after 2100, without overshooting the long-run radiative forcing target level (Clarke et al. 2007; Smith and Wigley 2006; Wise et al. 2009).

Worst Case Emission Scenario

Under this scenario, greenhouse gas emissions continue to increase over time, representative of scenarios in the literature that lead to high greenhouse gas concentration levels (Riahi et al. 2007).

Modeling

Background

Landscape suitability was modeled used 1) occurrence data derived from the Botanical Information and Ecology Network (BIEN), 2) digital maps of climate, and 3) a statistical model to associate these two types of information. We describe each of these in more detail.


Occurrence data are records of a species occurring in a particular place,i.e., latitude and longitude. They are typically derived from natural history collections (herbarium specimens), but in the case of BIEN, also include the locations of U.S. Forest Service forest inventory plots (Forest Inventory and Analysis, FIA), and other published sources of data (see BIEN).


Two types of digital maps of climate were used – current climate and future climate. Current climate data were used to build a model of a species’ climatic niche and predict its geographic distribution; future climate data were used to predict the species’ geographic distribution under future climate scenarios. We used digital maps of climate from two sources, worldclim and NASA. Both are statistically- downscaled and bias-corrected, based on the latest climate modeling results from the IPCC (AR5). The climate variables used were the 19 bioclim variables, described here, which capture major dimensions of climate expected to be important for the survival, growth, and reproduction of many organisms – mean annual temperature, mean annual precipitation, precipitation in the hottest three months of the year, mean temperature in the coldest three months of the year, etc.


Statistical modeling of climatic niches and geographical distributions was conducted using the freely- available algorithm Maxent9. Maxent employs a maximum entropy method to model a species' distribution; it has performed well in comparisons among species distribution modeling methods10. In essense, Maxent uses the data on what climate is like where the species is known to occur to build a statistical model that predicts the climatic suitability of the rest of the landscape for that species.
